# 10 — Debt Awareness (One Chapter, Not the Whole Book)

You said debt payment is your *last* priority — that learning and growing gradually come first. This file respects that. It's not a debt-killer plan. It's an **awareness** document so you understand what you're carrying, can monitor it, and know when (much later) it'll start to address itself as a side-effect of everything else working.

**Read this once now. Re-read it every 3 months.** Don't dwell on it daily.

---

## What you're carrying

Approximately **7,00,000 BDT** across four buckets:

| Bucket | Rate | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Credit card | ~24–30% p.a., compounds monthly | Grows if minimum payment isn't met |
| Personal loan | ~9–15% p.a., fixed EMI | Pays itself off on a schedule if EMI is met |
| Family / informal | 0% | No financial cost, social cost only |
| Market / chit-fund | Often **5%/month = 60%+ APR** | Grows fast if not actively paid down |

These are typical Bangladesh rates. Your actual numbers may vary — get the exact rates from your loan papers / SMS notifications and put them in the **Debt tab** of `tracker.html`. **Just know them. Don't act on them yet.**

---

## What each one really is, in plain language

### Credit card (24–30% APR)
This is borrowing the bank's money at a high rate, hoping to pay it back next month. As long as you pay the full balance each month, the interest is zero. If you carry a balance, the 24–30% kicks in and compounds. Most people who use credit cards as "extra money" end up paying interest on interest for years.

**Right action for Stage 0–1:** stop adding new charges to it. Put the card in a drawer. Pay the minimum every month so it doesn't damage your credit. Don't attack the balance yet.

### Personal loan (9–15% APR)
A bank lent you a lump sum at a fixed rate, and you agreed to pay it back in fixed monthly EMIs. This is the most "honest" of your loans — the bank doesn't surprise you. The EMI runs on schedule, principal shrinks each month, and one day it ends.

**Right action for Stage 0–1:** keep paying the EMI on time. Don't accelerate. Don't worry about it. The schedule will run.

### Family / informal (0%)
You owe someone you know. Maybe a parent, a sibling, a friend, an uncle. There's no interest — but there *is* a relationship at stake. People remember being lent money. They remember not getting it back. They remember silence even more than the missing amount.

**Right action:** even if you can only pay 2,000 BDT/month, *pay something*. Tell them upfront: *"I can only do 2,000/month for now, but I will keep paying — never silently disappear from this."* That conversation alone is worth more than the amount. The relationship is wealth.

### Market / chit-fund (often 60%+ APR)
This is the dangerous one. Often comes from informal lenders, neighborhood committees, money clubs (chit funds), or business associates. The "5% per month" sounds small. It is not. Multiplied across 12 months it's 79.6% effective annual rate (with compounding). Left unchecked, this loan can *double* in less than 18 months.

**Right action for Stage 0–1:** at minimum, **pay enough each month to cover the interest** — i.e., don't let the principal grow. If the loan is 1,00,000 BDT at 5%/month, that means paying at least 5,000/month just to hold steady. Anything below that means the loan is silently swallowing your future surplus.

If you can't even cover the interest right now, this is the one debt that should make you uncomfortable. Not panic. Not war. *Uncomfortable enough to address it actively by Stage 2 at the latest.*

## Why we're not panicking about it

A few honest reasons it's OK to address debt slowly:

1. **You're still earning.** Income coming in monthly is the most important variable. As long as freelance work continues, the foundation is there.
2. **Family loans (0%) and personal loans (single-digit %)** aren't urgent. They run on autopilot.
3. **Credit card** can be neutralized by not using it. The balance freezes (sort of) once the minimum is paid.
4. **Learning grows faster than debt usually destroys** — for most rates. The exception is the chit-fund one, which is why it deserves its own monthly check.

The math: a freelancer with rising income who learns finance and gradually builds savings + investing habit usually clears debt by Year 3 *as a side-effect* — not as a sacrifice. The exception is very-high-rate informal debt, which sometimes needs earlier action.

---

## The "slow accelerate" approach

This was the path you chose, and it's reasonable. Here's what it looks like:

### Stage 0 (Months 1–2) — Awareness only
- Fill in the Debt tab with exact numbers
- Don't pay extra anywhere
- Make sure the chit-fund loan at minimum gets its interest paid each month
- Continue learning

### Stage 1 (Month 2–3) — Tracking confirms reality
- See your real surplus from 30 days of tracking
- If chit-fund interest is being missed, this is when you discover it and fix it
- Still no big extras

### Stage 2 (Month 3–9) — Income grows, small extras start
- As freelance income rises from rate-raises + niche work, surplus grows
- **Start applying 30–50% of surplus to the chit-fund loan**
- Reason: 60% APR is high enough that "slow accelerate" still means actively paying it down — the alternative is unfair to your future self
- Credit card, personal loan: still on minimums/schedule
- Family loan: keep the token payment going

### Stage 3 (Month 6–18) — Investing begins, debt accelerates further
- Even as you start small DPS / Sanchaypatra, the chit-fund debt should be getting visibly smaller each month
- Credit card payoff starts seriously here — the rate gap (30% vs your investments at 11%) means it's now worth attacking
- Personal loan continues on schedule
- Family loan: as cash flow improves, increase the token amount

### Stage 4–5 (Year 2+) — Mostly cleared
- The chit-fund loan should be near zero
- Credit card off
- Personal loan running out its natural schedule
- Family loan paid faster or fully

By Year 3, if the plan ran, you're debt-free — without ever having made it the central drama of your life.

## How to monitor without obsessing

In the tracker:

- **Debt tab** — visit once a month, on the 1st, to update outstanding balances. That's it.
- **Debt snapshot history** — log the monthly total. Watch the line move.
- **Avalanche simulator** (in Debt tab) — once Stage 2 starts, try different "extra surplus per month" amounts to see how it changes the payoff timeline. This is a *what-if tool*, not a commitment.

Monthly check-in only. **Don't make debt a daily thought.** Daily is for learning and tracking expenses, not for staring at debt numbers.

---

## When the chit-fund loan deserves emergency attention

There's one scenario where the "slow accelerate" approach gets overridden:

**If the chit-fund loan is growing faster than you can keep up with**, even after Stage 1 tracking — it's time to act sooner.

Signs:
- The principal hasn't gone down in 6+ months despite payments
- The lender is putting pressure on you / your family
- The interest payment alone is eating more than 20% of your monthly income
- You're borrowing from another source to pay this lender

If 2+ of those are true, talk to a Bangladesh Bank-listed banking ombudsman or a trusted financial advisor — informal loans in Bangladesh sometimes can be restructured, settled at a discount, or even legally challenged if the rate is usurious. **Don't suffer in silence.**
