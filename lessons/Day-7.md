## 2. The concept

### The core prohibitions

Islamic finance is defined by what it forbids. Three things are core:

#### 1. Riba (সুদ) — Prohibition of interest

> **Riba** is any predetermined excess paid to a lender for the use of money, with no underlying productive risk-sharing.

The classical example: you lend 1,00,000 BDT to someone, and they agree to pay back 1,10,000 in a year. That extra 10,000 — paid simply for the passage of time, not because of any shared risk — is *riba al-nasi'a* (the most common form). It's prohibited in Islam.

The deeper logic: money by itself is not productive. It only becomes productive when combined with effort, risk, and a real economic activity. Earning a return just because you had money (no effort, no risk) violates the Islamic principle that wealth should flow from real productive activity.

**Modern examples of riba:**
- Conventional savings account interest (4% on your balance)
- Bank fixed deposit interest
- Credit card interest charges
- Conventional bond coupons
- Personal loan interest
- Mortgage interest

#### 2. Gharar (অতিরিক্ত অনিশ্চয়তা) — Excessive uncertainty

> **Gharar** is excessive ambiguity or uncertainty in a contract.

Some uncertainty in business is unavoidable and acceptable. But contracts where the terms, the goods, the price, or the outcome are deeply unclear are forbidden.

**Modern examples of gharar:**
- Conventional insurance (premium pays for an uncertain future event, structured as a one-sided transfer)
- Forex speculation (you're not trading real goods, just betting on price moves)
- Most derivatives, futures, and options
- Gambling in any form
- Lottery tickets

#### 3. Maysir (জুয়া) — Gambling / pure speculation

> **Maysir** is gain achieved purely by chance, without productive economic activity.

Closely related to gharar but specifically about chance-based gain.

**Modern examples of maysir:**
- Casino games
- Lottery
- Day trading where you're not analyzing fundamentals, just betting on short-term moves
- Cryptocurrency trading (controversial — some scholars say crypto itself is haram due to gharar/maysir, others say it depends on use case)

### Additional prohibitions

- **Investment in haram industries**: alcohol, pork, gambling, conventional banking/insurance, weapons, adult entertainment, etc.
- **Riba al-fadl**: unequal exchange of like-for-like commodities (technical; less relevant for personal finance)

### Halal alternatives — how Islamic finance works

The core innovation: instead of lending at interest, Islamic finance uses **partnership and trade structures** where the lender shares both profit and loss.

#### Mudaraba — Profit-sharing partnership

> One party provides capital (*rab-al-mal*), the other provides effort/expertise (*mudarib*). They agree to share *profit* by a ratio (e.g., 60/40). Losses are borne entirely by the capital provider; the worker loses their time and effort.

**Modern application:** Islamic banks like IBBL operate Mudaraba savings/term deposit accounts. You (depositor) are the capital provider. The bank is the mudarib. The bank invests your money in halal businesses, and you share in the profits — not at a guaranteed rate, but as a share of actual returns. The rate is *expected* but not contractually guaranteed (which is what makes it halal).

#### Musharaka — Joint partnership

> Both parties contribute capital AND share in the management. Profits shared by agreed ratio, losses shared in proportion to capital contribution.

**Modern application:** used for business partnerships, home financing (where the bank and you co-own a property), and joint ventures. The bank doesn't "lend" you money to buy a house — it *buys* part of the house with you, then you gradually buy out its share.

#### Sukuk — Islamic "bonds"

> Sukuk are certificates of *ownership* in real assets or productive ventures. You own a piece of something tangible, and you receive a share of the rent / profit it generates.

**Difference from conventional bonds:** a conventional bond is a loan with interest. A sukuk is fractional ownership of a productive asset. You're entitled to the asset's returns, but you also bear its risk.

**Modern application:** Bangladesh has issued sovereign sukuk. Many Islamic banks offer sukuk-based investment products. Globally, sukuk market is hundreds of billions of dollars.

#### Murabaha — Cost-plus sale

> The bank buys an asset you want, then sells it to you at a marked-up price, payable in installments. Critically: it's a *sale*, not a loan.

**Modern application:** Islamic auto financing — the bank actually buys the car, then sells it to you at a higher fixed price you pay in monthly installments. No interest, just a marked-up sale.

**Critical question:** is this *really* different from a conventional loan? Scholars debate. The form is different (sale vs lending), the substance often resembles interest. Most major scholars accept it as halal *if* the bank takes actual ownership for at least a moment and bears the asset's risk during that time.

#### Ijara — Leasing

> The bank buys an asset and leases it to you. Like a long-term rental. You make payments, and at the end you may or may not own the asset (depending on the contract type).

**Modern application:** equipment financing, vehicle leasing, sometimes home financing (Ijara Muntahia Bittamlik = lease ending in ownership).

#### Wadiah — Safekeeping

> Pure deposit with no return guaranteed. You give the bank money for safekeeping; they may use it but owe you the full amount back on demand. Any "gift" (hibah) from the bank to depositors is at the bank's discretion, not contractual.

**Modern application:** some basic Islamic checking accounts.

### How to evaluate "Is this product really halal?"

Many products are *labeled* Islamic without being substantively different from conventional ones. To check:

1. **What's the underlying contract?** Mudaraba, musharaka, murabaha, ijara, sukuk — these are the legitimate structures. If the brochure doesn't say which one, ask.

2. **Is there a Shariah board overseeing it?** Reputable Islamic banks have Shariah Supervisory Boards of qualified scholars. If a product isn't under such oversight, be cautious.

3. **Is the return guaranteed or expected?** A *guaranteed* fixed return on a deposit is structurally riba. An *expected* return (with the disclaimer that it depends on actual underlying business performance) is halal.

4. **Who bears the risk?** In legitimate Islamic finance, the capital provider bears risk. If a product promises capital protection AND a fixed return — it's mimicking conventional interest, which scholars disagree about.

5. **What does the underlying activity look like?** Mudaraba savings are halal because the bank invests in halal businesses. Sukuk are halal because they're tied to real productive assets. If you can't identify the underlying real economic activity, be cautious.

6. **AAOIFI compliance.** AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) sets the standards. Products certified AAOIFI-compliant have rigorous oversight. This is the gold standard in Islamic finance.

---

### Areas of ongoing scholarly debate

Not everything is settled. Educated Muslims often disagree on:

- **Conventional bank stocks** — some scholars allow buying stocks of conventional banks if revenue from interest is below a threshold (often 5%); others say no
- **Cryptocurrency** — some scholars say haram (excessive gharar, no real underlying asset); others say it can be halal depending on use case
- **Stock market participation generally** — most accept buying shares of halal businesses; some are stricter on screening
- **Insurance** — conventional insurance is broadly considered haram; Takaful (Islamic mutual insurance, where participants pool funds to help each other) is the halal alternative, but Takaful options in Bangladesh are still developing

When in doubt, consult a qualified scholar or stick to the most conservative position until you have more information. **"I'm not sure" is a valid reason to pass on a financial product.**

## 3. Worked examples

### Example A — IBBL Mudaraba Savings Account

You deposit 10,000 BDT.

**Conventional bank:** They guarantee 4% per year. You get 400 BDT regardless of bank performance. **Structurally:** lending at interest → riba.

**IBBL Mudaraba:** They expect ~6% per year, but it's a *profit share*. The bank invests your money (and others') in halal businesses. At year-end, they calculate actual profit, give you your share. In a great year, maybe you earn 7%. In a bad year, maybe 5% or less. **Structurally:** partnership in business → halal.

**The catch:** in practice, IBBL's Mudaraba rates have been very stable, mimicking conventional deposits. Some scholars worry this is "halal in form, conventional in substance." But the *contract* is legitimate, and the bank does have a Shariah board. Most scholars accept it.

### Example B — A "Forex Trading is Halal" claim

Someone says: "Forex trading is halal because I'm trading real currencies."

**Analysis:**
- Spot forex (immediate physical exchange of currencies) → generally halal
- Leveraged retail forex trading (margin trading, holding positions overnight with swap fees) → almost universally considered haram due to:
  - **Riba** (overnight swap fees are interest)
  - **Gharar** (excessive uncertainty, high leverage)
  - **Maysir** (pure speculation on short-term moves)

**Verdict:** the "I'm trading real currencies" defense doesn't hold up. Modern retail forex trading is haram by nearly all mainstream scholars. Add to that: it's also illegal in Bangladesh and a financial loss-maker for 90%+ of retail traders (from Day 6). Triple reason to avoid.

### Example C — "Islamic" credit card

A bank advertises an "Islamic credit card."

**How can it possibly be halal?** Conventional credit cards charge interest on unpaid balances — clear riba.

**The Islamic version** typically works on Murabaha or Ujrah (fee-based):
- You make purchases, the bank pays the merchant
- You owe the bank the amount
- If you pay on time → no extra charge
- If you pay late → a fee (not interest, structured as a penalty / administrative charge)

**Critical question:** is the "late fee" really different from interest? Scholars debate. Some argue if the late fee accrues regularly and proportionally to the unpaid amount, it's interest in disguise. Others accept it if it's a flat fee.

**Practical guidance:** if you're going to use a credit card at all, an "Islamic" version is probably better than a conventional one. But even better: don't carry credit card balances at all. The structural debate disappears if you pay in full every month.

### Example D — Sanchaypatra (Bangladesh government savings certificate)

5-Year Bangladesh Sanchaypatra. Pays ~11% per year.

**Is this halal?**

This is contested. The government pays a fixed predetermined return — which structurally looks like interest. Some scholars consider it haram for this reason.

Others argue that:
- It's a sovereign financial instrument
- The "interest" is partly a subsidy / welfare mechanism
- It's permissible under certain interpretations of dar-ul-harb / dar-ul-Islam frameworks (though Bangladesh doesn't strictly fit either)

**Common practical position:** many Bangladeshi Muslims do use Sanchaypatra. Others prefer to stick strictly to Mudaraba-based products. If you want to be strictly conservative, use IBBL/Al-Arafah Mudaraba Term Deposits instead.

**No definitive answer here.** This is a case where you should consult a scholar you trust. The course's role is to make you aware of the question, not to answer it for you.

### Example E — DSE blue-chip stocks

You want to buy 1,000 BDT of Square Pharma stock.

**Halal screening:**
1. Is Square Pharma's primary business halal? → Yes (pharmaceuticals)
2. Does it have significant haram revenue streams? → Need to check (interest from cash holdings, etc.)
3. Is its debt/asset ratio acceptable? → Common screen: total interest-bearing debt < 33% of total assets

For most major DSE blue-chips, these screens pass with the standard AAOIFI / Dow Jones Islamic Market screening. You can find Shariah-compliance lists from local Islamic finance advisors.

**Verdict:** likely halal. Worth confirming through formal screening before significant investment.

## 4. Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: Assuming all Islamic banks are equally Shariah-compliant

Not all Islamic banks have equally rigorous Shariah boards or oversight. Some have stricter implementations than others. Do basic due diligence.

### Mistake 2: Treating "Islamic" as a marketing label

A product labeled "Islamic" isn't automatically halal. Always ask about the underlying contract structure.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring halal options assuming they're worse

Many Muslims default to conventional banks because "it's easier" — but Islamic alternatives in Bangladesh are mature and comparable. The slight differences in rates / convenience are often worth it for the peace of mind.

### Mistake 4: Being so strict you never invest

The flip side: some people find every halal product to have *some* scholarly debate, so they never invest at all. They keep money in cash, lose 9% to inflation, and stay poor. **Following a mainstream-acceptable position is better than paralysis.** Pick a position you can justify, and proceed.

### Mistake 5: Mixing your conventional and Islamic accounts casually

If you maintain both for any reason (work salary at a conventional bank, etc.), be intentional. Most scholars suggest: receive salary in conventional bank if required, but accumulate / invest savings through Islamic channels.

### Mistake 6: Forgetting that Islamic finance has the same risk-return tradeoffs

Halal doesn't mean risk-free. A Mudaraba account can have a low-profit year. A halal stock can decline. A sukuk issuer can default. Apply Day 6's risk-return framework to halal options too. Don't assume "Islamic = safe."

## 🕌 The Islamic Perspective — Expanded

Today's lesson already integrates Islamic teaching throughout, but for the closing day of the course, let's go a little deeper into the Sunnah and the lives of the Sahaba (RA) — to show that what you've learned isn't just a theoretical framework, but a *lived practice* with 1,400 years of working models.

### The Prophet's ﷺ daily dua against financial harm

Earlier we mentioned the Prophet's ﷺ supplication for halal sufficiency. Here it is in full, with the situation in which he taught it:

**Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA) reported:** A man came to me and said: "I have to make a payment which I am unable to make." Ali (RA) told him: "Shall I not teach you words which the Messenger of Allah ﷺ taught me, with which, if you owed a debt as large as Mount Sayr, Allah would arrange for it to be paid off for you?" The man said: "Of course!" Ali (RA) said: "Say:

> *"Allahumma akfini bi halalika 'an haramika, wa aghnini bi fadlika 'amman siwak."*
>
> *"O Allah, suffice me with what You have made halal so I have no need for what You have made haram, and enrich me by Your bounty so I have no need for anyone besides You."*

(Sunan al-Tirmidhi 3563, classed Hasan)

**This is the single most relevant dua for everyone in this course.** Whatever financial situation you're in — debt, low income, unstable freelance work, family pressures — this is what the Prophet ﷺ taught a struggling believer to say. Memorize it. Recite it after every prayer. Especially during this learning journey.

### Another Prophetic dua for financial well-being

> *"Allahumma inni a'udhu bika min al-hammi wa al-hazani, wa al-'ajzi wa al-kasali, wa al-bukhli wa al-jubni, wa dala'id-dayni wa ghalabatir-rijal."*
>
> *"O Allah, I seek refuge in You from worry and grief, from incapacity and laziness, from miserliness and cowardice, from the burden of debt and being overpowered by men."*

(Sahih Bukhari 6369)

**Notice what the Prophet ﷺ groups together:** the *burden of debt* is mentioned alongside grief, weakness, and cowardice. The spiritual harm of being trapped in debt is real and recognized in the Sunnah — which is why our roadmap addresses debt as something to gradually unwind, not ignore.

### Khadijah (RA) — the businesswoman who funded Islam

We mentioned her on Day 5. Here's what's worth lingering on for Day 7's lens:

When the Prophet ﷺ received the first revelation in the cave of Hira and came home shaking and afraid, it was **Khadijah (RA)** — using both her wisdom and her wealth — who covered him, comforted him, took him to her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal to confirm what had happened, and then **funded the entirety of the early Islamic mission** for the next 10+ years.

Without her business success, the early years of Islam would have had no financial cushion. The wealth she had built through honest trade became the *operational backbone* of the world's most important spiritual movement.

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> *"Allah has not given me a better wife than Khadijah."* (Musnad Ahmad)

And years after her death, when he received a gift, he would send portions to her old friends — saying *"these were her friends."* The dignity she gained was eternal because it was rooted in honest wealth, used for righteous purposes.

### Abdul Rahman bin Awf (RA) — Mudaraba in action

We told his arrival story on Day 5 — "show me the market." Let's see the *kind of business he built*:

Abdul Rahman (RA) was famous for using **Mudaraba and Musharaka partnerships** in his trade. He would partner with other Sahaba — they'd contribute capital or expertise, agree on profit-share ratios, and trade together. He didn't borrow money at interest (riba — forbidden). He didn't lend at interest. He *partnered*.

When his caravans returned to Madinah, you could hear them coming from miles away — 700 camels laden with goods, the sound shaking the city. His business was so profitable that he could fund armies, free slaves, and feed thousands during famines — all from honest trade partnerships.

**This is the practical example of why Mudaraba/Musharaka is the heart of Islamic finance.** Risk shared. Profit shared. Effort respected. Capital respected. No riba.

When Abdul Rahman (RA) died at age 72, his estate was so vast that the inheritance of just his four wives (each receiving 1/8 each, then divided) was reportedly *80,000 dinars per wife*. Yet his last act — before death — was to free 30 slaves. The Prophet ﷺ said of him:

> *"O Allah, give Abdul Rahman bin Awf to drink from the Salsabil of Paradise."* (Sahih traditions)

This is the model: **wealth earned through halal partnerships, used to liberate others and serve Allah's cause.**

### Uthman ibn Affan (RA) — the strategic philanthropist

We mentioned the well of Ruma on Day 5. Three more lessons from Uthman's (RA) financial life worth knowing:

1. **The Army of Distress (Jaysh al-Usrah)** — The Battle of Tabuk required outfitting an army during severe drought. The Prophet ﷺ called for contributions. Uthman (RA) gave **900 camels and 100 horses fully equipped, plus 1,000 gold dinars**. The Prophet ﷺ said:
   > *"Whatever Uthman does after this day will not harm him."* (Sunan al-Tirmidhi 3701)

2. **He systematized waqf (charitable endowment)** — well-resourced believers buying productive assets and dedicating their income permanently to community benefit. This is the original "endowment" model used today by universities and foundations — Uthman (RA) pioneered the structure 14 centuries earlier.

3. **He set the model of being a wealthy ruler without using office for gain** — as Caliph, he refused state stipends for himself, living from his pre-Caliphate trade wealth. Public service without using it for personal enrichment.

### Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) — wealth's highest purpose

When Abu Bakr (RA) gave everything for the Tabuk campaign:

> The Prophet ﷺ asked: *"What have you left for your family?"*
> Abu Bakr (RA) replied: *"I have left for them Allah and His Messenger."* (Sunan Abi Dawud 1678)

And on his death-bed, Abu Bakr (RA) instructed his daughter Aisha (RA): *"Return whatever I took from the bayt al-mal (treasury) above what's needed for my burial."* Even on his last day, his concern was the *amanah* (trust) of public wealth.

### The Sahaba's overall financial pattern

Look at the Sahaba's relationship with wealth and notice the pattern:

| Stage | Approach |
|---|---|
| Pre-Islam many of them were poor or modest | Worked, traded, built |
| After Islam they earned through halal means | Trade, partnerships, war booty distribution |
| As wealth grew | Increased zakat, sadaqah, supporting Islam |
| As community needed | Funded armies, freed slaves, built infrastructure |
| At death | Most had given away the majority |

The wealth wasn't the goal. It was the *tool*. The goal was Allah's pleasure and the Ummah's benefit.

### The Prophet ﷺ's distinctive approach to debt

The Prophet ﷺ took debt very seriously. There are multiple authentic narrations showing:

- He refused to perform the funeral prayer over a deceased person who had unpaid debts (until Abu Qatada RA volunteered to pay them). (Sahih Bukhari 2289)
- He prayed against debt frequently
- He warned that the soul of a believer is "suspended" by their debt until it's paid (Sunan Ibn Majah 2413, Hasan)

**Why?** Because debt creates dependencies that compromise *amanah* (trust) and `aql` (sound judgment). A heavily indebted person becomes susceptible to bad decisions, lies, broken promises — the Prophet ﷺ said exactly this. (See Day 2's Islamic Perspective on this hadith.)

This is why our roadmap addresses debt — gradually, with awareness — even though the user's chosen priority is learning-first. *Knowing* about debt's spiritual weight is part of the learning. Eventually clearing it is part of the journey.
