# Day 6 — Risk vs Return

Today you build a scam detector. Permanently. Anyone showing you an investment, a "scheme," a "tip," or a "guaranteed return" will be processed through the framework you learn today. After this lesson, the noise of the financial world becomes a lot quieter, because most of what's being sold to you reveals itself as either bad math or outright fraud.

---

## 1. Why this matters

A Facebook ad: *"30% monthly returns through forex! Send only 50,000 BDT to get started. I made 5 lakh in my first 3 months!"*

Without today's lesson, you might think: "Maybe? Worth a shot?"

With today's lesson, you'll instantly know: 30% per month = 2,229% per year (compounded). That's so high it would mean the person could turn 50,000 BDT into approximately 11 *crore* in a single year. If this worked, the person posting wouldn't be on Facebook trying to recruit you for 50k — they'd already own a private island.

It's a scam. Always. Without exception.

That's just one example. Today's lesson is the systematic framework — not just for spotting forex scams, but for evaluating *every* investment, opportunity, business pitch, and "deal" that will come at you for the rest of your life.

The framework has two parts:
1. **The risk-return spectrum** — where any opportunity sits, and what return is plausible at that risk
2. **The scam detection checklist** — what red flags reveal that something isn't what it claims

By the end of today, both will be permanently installed.

---

## 2. The concept

### The fundamental law of finance

> **Higher returns require higher risk. There are no exceptions.**

This is as close to a universal law as exists in finance. It's not "usually true." It's "always true, in proportion."

If anyone offers you a return higher than you'd typically get from the broader market at that risk level, they're either:
1. **Wrong** (don't understand the risk they're taking)
2. **Lucky** (got a result that won't repeat)
3. **Lying** (a scam, a Ponzi, or wishful sales pitch)

There is no fourth option called "genuine, sustainable, low-risk high return." It doesn't exist. If it did, every institutional investor in the world would already be using it, and you wouldn't be hearing about it from a relative's WhatsApp message.

### The risk-return spectrum

Here's the spectrum, with realistic Bangladesh examples for each level:

#### Tier 1: Essentially Risk-Free (3-7% nominal)
- Bank savings accounts (3-4%)
- Sukuk / government bonds
- Bangladesh Bank deposits
- Mudaraba savings at major Islamic banks (5-6%)

You're effectively lending money to a very safe entity. Your money is *very* unlikely to disappear. But it barely beats (or loses to) inflation.

#### Tier 2: Very Low Risk (7-11% nominal)
- DPS / FDR (Mudaraba Term Deposit) at established banks (~7-10%)
- 5-Year Bangladesh Sanchaypatra (~11%)
- High-grade corporate sukuk
- Money market funds

Small lock-in period or some restrictions, but principal is nearly guaranteed by major institutions or government.

#### Tier 3: Low to Medium Risk (10-15% expected, long-term)
- Diversified blue-chip Bangladeshi stocks held 10+ years (Square Pharma, BATBC, Renata, GP, etc.) — *average* return ~12-15%
- Diversified halal equity index funds (Wahed, etc.)
- Real estate in established Dhaka areas — *long-term* return roughly inflation + 3-5%

Year-to-year fluctuation can be big (a stock might drop 30% in a year and recover later), but *long-term* and *diversified*, returns are meaningfully better than Tier 1-2.

#### Tier 4: Medium to High Risk (15-25% expected, but variable)
- Concentrated single-stock positions
- Small-cap stocks in DSE
- Speculative real estate (flipping, undeveloped land)
- Buying into a friend's small business
- Cryptocurrency held long-term (controversial — also illegal in Bangladesh)

Outcomes vary enormously. Some years +50%, some years −50%. Long-term average for *skilled* investors might reach 18-20%. Long-term average for *unskilled* investors is much worse — often negative.

#### Tier 5: Very High Risk / Gambling (could be anything from −100% to massive gain)
- Active forex trading
- Day trading individual stocks
- Crypto trading (not holding — trading)
- Lottery tickets
- Trying to predict short-term market moves
- Most "schemes"

Expected long-term return for retail participants: **negative**. Most lose. A few get lucky. The lucky ones post on social media. The losers stay silent. This is survivorship bias in action.

#### Tier 6: Fraud / Ponzi territory (advertised 30%+ monthly, "guaranteed")
- MLM schemes that pay early entrants from later entrants' deposits
- "Forex signals" / "trading bots" / "managed accounts" with guaranteed returns
- "Investment clubs" your relative is now promoting heavily
- WhatsApp / Telegram "exclusive opportunities"

These have a mathematical inevitability: anyone who joins early gets paid (for a while). Anyone who joins late loses everything. The system collapses when new deposits can't cover old withdrawals.

---

### How to use the spectrum

When anyone offers you anything:

1. **Locate it on the spectrum.** What tier does the *claimed* return suggest?
2. **Locate the *actual* risk.** Is the risk being honestly disclosed?
3. **If claimed return > what that tier delivers** → red flag. The claim is too good for the stated risk.

Example: someone offers you "12-15% return on lending to my small business." That's a Tier 3 / Tier 4 risk (small business lending — they could fail). 12-15% is plausible for that risk level. **Plausible** doesn't mean "do it" — but the math isn't fishy.

Example: someone offers "12-15% per *month* on lending to their business." That's claiming Tier 5+ returns. 144-180% annualized. **Impossible for a legitimate business to pay sustainably.** Either they're using new money to pay old money (Ponzi), or they're lying about their numbers.

---

### Volatility vs Permanent Loss

A subtle but crucial distinction:

**Volatility** = the price goes up and down. Doesn't necessarily mean you lose. If you hold through dips, you still get the long-term return.

**Permanent loss** = the asset itself fails. The company goes bankrupt. The scheme collapses. You don't recover.

A diversified portfolio of blue-chip stocks: **high volatility, low permanent-loss risk**. (Even if 1-2 companies fail, the portfolio survives.)

A single small-cap stock: **high volatility, meaningful permanent-loss risk**. (The one company can fail entirely.)

A Ponzi scheme: **looks like low volatility until it collapses, then 100% permanent loss**. (The most dangerous combination — feels safe right up until catastrophe.)

**Most beginners are terrified of volatility and underestimate permanent loss.** They sell stocks during a dip (volatility scared them) and then buy into Ponzi schemes (low volatility right up until total loss). The opposite is correct: tolerate volatility in *good* investments; avoid anything with permanent-loss risk.

---

### Survivorship bias

You'll hear "my friend invested in [X] and made 5 lakh in 6 months."

You **never** hear "my friend invested in [Y] and lost everything." Because:
1. People don't post about their losses
2. Losers often disappear from the community
3. The wins are publicized, the losses are private

This means **everything you hear about "great investments" is filtered through a winner-survivor lens.** The 100 people who lost everything in the same scheme are invisible. The 1 who won shows up on Facebook with a Mercedes photo.

**Defense:** assume that for every "winner" you hear about in a non-broad-market investment, there are 10-50 silent losers. If you wouldn't take the bet at those odds, don't take it because of one visible winner.

## 3. Worked examples

### Example A — Evaluating a "lend to my friend's business at 18% per year" offer

Friend's business: 4-year-old shop. Friend wants to expand. Offers you 18% per year on a 2-year loan, paid quarterly.

**Risk analysis:**
- Small business default risk: meaningful (maybe 15-25% of small businesses in Bangladesh fail in any given 5-year window)
- Friend is trustworthy but not infallible
- Your money is locked up — no early withdrawal
- 18% per year is a Tier 3-4 return

**Plausibility:** Yes. 18% is plausible compensation for the risk you're taking. The math is honest.

**Should you do it?** Depends:
- Can you afford to lose this money? (If it's 50% of your savings — no. If it's 5% of your savings — maybe.)
- Do you trust the friend AND the business?
- Have you seen the business's actual financials?
- Is there a written agreement?

**Verdict:** plausible offer, but only at a small portion of your wealth, with full information. Pass otherwise.

### Example B — The "forex / signals" scam

Telegram group: "We share trading signals. Last month our group made average 40% return. Join for 5,000 BDT/month subscription."

**Math:**
- 40% per month = 5,599% per year compounded
- Tier 6 territory. Impossible.

**Where the money actually comes from:**
- Subscriptions from new members (the real "product")
- Affiliate commissions from forex brokers (who profit from your losing trades)
- Many of the "winning trade" screenshots are faked or cherry-picked

**Verdict:** scam. Walk away. Block the contact. Don't engage to "see how it works" — engagement leads to harder pitches.

### Example C — A relative's MLM pitch

Relative says: "I joined this network. You buy a 30,000 BDT 'starter kit,' then recruit 3 people who each buy a kit, and you earn 5,000 from each recruit. Plus your downline keeps earning."

**Math:**
- For everyone in the group to make money, the network must grow exponentially
- 3 people → 9 → 27 → 81 → 243 → 729 → 2,187 (level 7)
- Within 8-10 levels, you've recruited more people than the population of Dhaka
- Mathematically impossible to sustain → someone at the bottom always loses

**The product** ("kit") is usually overpriced trinkets or low-quality goods. The real "product" is the recruitment.

**Verdict:** classic pyramid / MLM. Even if technically not illegal, the math guarantees losses for most participants. Decline politely. Don't argue (relatives feel attacked). Just don't join.

### Example D — A "great" real estate deal

Real estate agent: "I have a plot in Purbachal, will appreciate 30% in 18 months. 8 lakh down payment now, balance later. Guaranteed."

**Analysis:**
- 30% in 18 months = ~19% annualized
- Bangladesh real estate has historically returned roughly inflation + 3-5% = ~12-14% per year long-term
- 19% is high but not impossible — if it's in a developing area with strong infrastructure plans
- "Guaranteed" appreciation is a **lie** — no investment has guaranteed price changes
- Liquidity is poor — you can't easily exit
- Lots of legal documentation risks in Bangladesh real estate

**Verdict:** the *number* is plausible, but the "guarantee" is fiction. Real estate has volatility, fraud risk (fake titles, double-sold plots), and illiquidity. Investigate the agent, the project, RAJUK approval, title chain — or pass.

### Example E — Your own DPS at IBBL

IBBL Mudaraba DPS: ~10% nominal expected.

- Locate on spectrum: Tier 2 (very low risk — bank-backed, Shariah-compliant)
- Realistic for tier? Yes — 7-11% is typical for Tier 2
- Volatility? Essentially none
- Permanent loss risk? Very low (IBBL is a major bank)
- "Hidden" downsides? Lock-in period, early withdrawal penalty

**Verdict:** a perfectly honest, modest, real return investment. The numbers match the tier. No red flags. *This is what a legitimate investment looks like* — boring, modest, reliable. Compare to the "exciting" offers above and notice how dramatically different the marketing energy is. **Boring is good.**

## 4. Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: Equating high return with smart investing

People sometimes brag about a 60% gain on a stock they picked. What they don't share is the 7 other stocks they bought that lost money. **Net portfolio return is what matters, not the best winner.**

### Mistake 2: Being scared of all risk

The opposite extreme: someone learns about scams and decides "all investing is risky, I'll keep everything in cash." Now they lose 9% to inflation every year. **Some risk is necessary** to beat inflation. The skill is calibrating risk, not avoiding it.

### Mistake 3: Trusting people more than systems

"My uncle would never cheat me." Maybe not intentionally. But your uncle can also be wrong about his investment. People often invest based on relationship trust ("I trust him") when they should invest based on system trust ("I understand the underlying business / instrument / risk").

### Mistake 4: Thinking "I'll just try a small amount"

Many people get sucked in this way. "Just 5,000 to test it." Then it "works" (the early entrants of a Ponzi always get paid). Then they put in 50,000. Then 5 lakh. Then they tell relatives. Then it collapses. **If you wouldn't invest 5 lakh in it, don't invest 5,000 either** — the small-amount entry is the scam's recruitment mechanism.

### Mistake 5: Following loud people instead of evidence

A loud, confident person promoting an investment scheme is *more* suspicious, not less. Real long-term wealth-builders are usually quiet about it. The people shouting on social media about their "investment system" are almost always selling courses, recruiting downlines, or running schemes.

### Mistake 6: Believing past performance guarantees future returns

"This stock has gone up 30% per year for 3 years." That's information. It is **not** a prediction. Past performance is famously a weak predictor of future returns. Especially for individual stocks or small markets. Every prospectus literally has to say this in fine print — and everyone ignores it.

## 5. Try this today (the 40-minute exercise)

### Step A — The Red Flag Checklist (10 min)

Print this or write it in your tracker's Notes tab. Any of these = walk away:

**The 7 Red Flags**

1. ⚠️ **Returns above 20-25% per year claimed as "typical"** — almost certainly impossible or unsustainable
2. ⚠️ **The word "guaranteed" attached to any number above the Sanchaypatra rate** — guarantees at high returns don't exist
3. ⚠️ **Pressure to "join now" / "limited spots" / "act fast"** — legitimate investments don't run on FOMO
4. ⚠️ **You earn more by recruiting others than from the product itself** — classic pyramid
5. ⚠️ **The "product" is mostly the system / training / kit (not a real underlying asset or business)** — Ponzi/MLM signal
6. ⚠️ **You can't easily understand where the returns come from** — if you can't explain the source of profit, you don't know what you're invested in
7. ⚠️ **Returns are consistently positive month after month, with no down periods** — real investments have ups and downs. Steady-high-returns = fraud (Madoff's classic giveaway)

If any 1 of these is present → walk away. If 3+ are present → run.

### Step B — Audit "investments" you've been pitched (15 min)

Think back: in the last year, what financial opportunities have been pitched to you? List them.

For each, ask:
1. What tier of the spectrum?
2. What return was claimed?
3. Is the claimed return plausible for that tier?
4. How many red flags from the checklist?
5. Decision (in hindsight): join, decline, monitor?

Example format:

| Pitch | Tier | Claimed return | Plausible? | Red flags | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Relative's MLM | T6 | 200%+/yr | No | 4-5 of 7 | Decline |
| IBBL DPS | T2 | 10%/yr | Yes | 0 | Join (in Stage 3) |
| Friend's business | T3 | 18%/yr | Yes | 0-1 (verify) | Maybe |

Write 3-5 examples in your tracker's Notes tab. Tag with `#day6-audit`.

### Step C — Build your "personal investment ladder" (10 min)

Based on the spectrum, what should YOUR investment ladder look like as you progress through stages?

| Stage | Tier | Examples | Approx % of monthly surplus to allocate here |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stage 3 (start) | T1-T2 | Mudaraba Savings, DPS, Sanchaypatra | 80-100% |
| Stage 3-4 | + T3 | DSE blue-chip halal stocks, sukuk | start adding 10-20% |
| Stage 4-5 | + T3 deeper | Diversified equity, international ETFs | adjust as comfort grows |
| Anywhere | NEVER | T5-T6 instruments | 0% — these aren't investments |

Write your own version. Adjust percentages based on your risk tolerance. *Crucially:* Tier 5-6 stays at 0% **forever**, regardless of stage. There's never a stage where forex trading becomes a good idea.

## 🕌 The Islamic Perspective

Everything you learned today — the risk-return spectrum, the scam detection framework, the seven red flags — Islam codified the underlying principles **1,400 years before modern financial regulation existed**. The categories of forbidden transactions in Islamic law map almost perfectly onto the modern categories of "scams" and "destructive speculation." This isn't coincidence — it's confirmation that Allah's wisdom anticipated exactly the financial pathologies we'd later face.

### Gharar — the divine prohibition of "Tier 5–6" structures

**Gharar** (الغرر) means "excessive uncertainty" or "deception by ambiguity." The Prophet ﷺ explicitly prohibited it:

> Abu Hurayrah (RA) reported: *"The Messenger of Allah ﷺ forbade the sale of gharar."* (Sahih Muslim 1513)

What's gharar in practice?
- Buying a fish before it's caught
- Selling crops before they're ripe (with full uncertainty)
- Contracts where the price, quantity, or delivery is unclear
- **Pure speculation on price movements with no underlying asset transfer**

Recognize that last one? It's exactly the structure of modern forex trading, day trading, binary options, leveraged crypto trading. **All Tier 5–6 instruments from today's lesson** — by the standard of classical Islamic jurisprudence, they are gharar. Forbidden 14 centuries ago, before anyone even invented the word "forex."

### Maysir — the gambling prohibition

In **Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:90–91)**:

> *"O you who believe! Intoxicants, gambling, idols, and divining arrows are abominations from the work of Satan. So avoid them, that you may be successful. Satan only wants to cause animosity and hatred among you through intoxicants and gambling and to turn you away from the remembrance of Allah and from prayer. So will you not desist?"*

Notice the categorization — Allah ﷻ places gambling alongside intoxicants and idol worship. Why so severe? Because gambling is wealth gained by chance, not by productive effort. It corrodes the entire economic moral fabric.

**What looks like Tier 5–6 from today's lesson is structurally maysir:**
- Day trading where you're just betting on short-term price moves
- "Forex signals" — paying to bet on currency direction
- Pump-and-dump groups
- Lottery, casino, sports betting
- Speculative crypto trading divorced from any project fundamentals

The Quran didn't list these specifically. It didn't have to. The *principle* — pure chance-based wealth without productive activity — was identified and forbidden.

### Eating wealth unjustly — the broad prohibition

> *"And do not consume one another's wealth unjustly..."* (Quran 2:188)

> *"O you who believe! Do not consume one another's wealth unjustly, but only [in lawful] business by mutual consent."* (Quran 4:29)

Every scam, every Ponzi, every MLM that promises returns that mathematically can't be sustained — they are all **unjust consumption of others' wealth**. The early entrants eat the deposits of the later entrants. That's not "investment" — it's the Quran's definition of forbidden transaction.

### The truthful merchant — the highest commercial dignity

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> *"The truthful and trustworthy merchant will be with the prophets, the truthful, and the martyrs on the Day of Judgment."* (Sunan al-Tirmidhi 1209, classed Sahih by al-Albani)

Think about who's listed here: **prophets, truthful witnesses, and martyrs**. The Prophet ﷺ placed honest traders in the company of the highest spiritual ranks. Why? Because honesty in commerce is so rare, so difficult, and so consequential — it requires the same character that prophethood does.

The flip side, the Prophet ﷺ said:

> *"Both parties [to a sale] have the option [to cancel] as long as they have not parted. If they speak the truth and make things clear, they will be blessed in their sale. But if they conceal anything or lie, the blessing of their sale will be wiped out."* (Sahih Bukhari 2079)

Transparency is the structural antidote to scams. **Islamic commercial law was the world's first systematic consumer protection framework** — built on the principle that both parties must know what they're agreeing to, with full disclosure.

### The Prophet's ﷺ own protection from financial harm

The Prophet ﷺ taught a beautiful supplication for protection from financial entanglements:

> *"Allahumma akfini bi halalika 'an haramika, wa aghnini bi fadlika 'amman siwak."*
> *"O Allah, suffice me with Your halal so I have no need for Your haram, and enrich me with Your bounty so I have no need of anyone besides You."* (Sunan al-Tirmidhi 3563, Hasan)

Why is this dua relevant to Day 6? Because the *psychological doorway* into scams is desperation or greed. When someone is financially stretched, they become susceptible to "guaranteed high returns" pitches. This dua addresses both — asking Allah for halal sufficiency (eliminating the desperation) and for independence from anyone (eliminating the greed for "easy" gains).

### The Sahaba's response to financial fitnah

When the Prophet ﷺ established the Madinan economy, the Companions immediately abandoned riba-based transactions and pre-Islamic gambling practices (which had been widespread). There's no recorded case of a Sahabi arguing "but this scheme is different" or "let me see if I can profit from this one." Their response was *sami'na wa ata'na* — "we hear and we obey."

This is the model: **when you understand a transaction is structurally forbidden, walk away regardless of how attractive it looks**. Don't try to engineer exceptions for yourself.

### The seven red flags — mapped to Islamic principles

| Today's Red Flag | Islamic principle violated |
|---|---|
| Returns above 20-25% claimed as typical | Likely riba or gharar — sustainable real returns from halal trade rarely exceed this |
| "Guaranteed" high returns | Riba — guarantees on capital-plus-return is structurally interest |
| FOMO / pressure to join now | Gharar (incomplete information) + ihtikar mindset (manipulating decisions) |
| Earning more from recruiting than from products | Eating wealth unjustly — recruits are the "product" |
| Returns from the system, not real assets | Gharar — no underlying real economic activity |
| You can't explain where returns come from | Lack of transparency (the Bukhari hadith above) |
| Consistently positive, no down periods | Likely fraud — real productive activity has fluctuations |

**Every red flag from modern scam detection maps onto something the Quran or Sunnah already prohibited.** When you follow the seven red flags, you're aligned with Islamic teaching even if you didn't realize it.

### Practical takeaway

Day 6 gave you the scam detector. Islam gave you something deeper: *a values framework that makes scams structurally unattractive in the first place*.

A Muslim who genuinely internalizes Islamic finance principles — *seeking halal sufficiency, avoiding gharar, avoiding maysir, trusting Allah's barakah over schemes* — is *naturally* protected from 99% of the financial fraud you encounter today. Not because they're smarter, but because they're operating from a different value system that recognizes "fast, easy, guaranteed money" as a spiritual red flag, not just a financial one.

The Quran told us this 1,400 years before Bernie Madoff was born:

> *"And what is the life of this world but a deceiving enjoyment?"* (Quran 3:185)

Scams work because they exploit our desire for shortcuts. Islam's antidote: there are no shortcuts to halal wealth. *Trade, partnership, honest work, patience, trust in Allah's barakah.* Boring is good. Slow is good. The Prophet ﷺ became Al-Amin not by genius schemes but by **consistent honest dealing over many years**.
