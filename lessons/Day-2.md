# Day 2 — Compound Interest

If you only mastered one concept in this entire course, this would be the one. Albert Einstein supposedly called compound interest "the most powerful force in the universe." Whether or not he actually said that, the math is unmistakable. By the end of today, you will understand why.

---

## 1. Why this matters

Two stories, same person, two universes.

**Universe A:** A 30-year-old in Dhaka deposits 2,000 BDT/month into a Shariah-compliant DPS that earns ~10% per year. He never increases the amount. Just 2,000 every month, on autopilot, for 30 years until he's 60.

At age 60, he has approximately **₹45,40,000 BDT** (about 45 lakh).

He deposited a total of 2,000 × 12 × 30 = **7,20,000 BDT** (about 7 lakh).

The other 38 lakh? Pure compound interest. Money he didn't earn — the system earned it for him.

**Universe B:** Same person, same age 30, takes 50,000 BDT on a credit card at 30% APR for a phone he didn't really need. He pays only the minimum each month (~5% of balance). He never adds more debt — just leaves that one 50,000 sitting there at 30%.

At age 60 — same 30 years — that one 50,000 he never paid off has cost him approximately **₹15,00,000 BDT** in interest. That's 15 *lakh* of his life, gone, for one impulse phone purchase that he probably forgot about by month 3.

Same force. Working for you in Universe A. Working against you in Universe B.

The single biggest factor separating wealthy people from broke people is whether compound interest is on their side or working against them.

## 2. The concept

### Simple interest vs compound interest

**Simple interest** is interest calculated only on the original amount.
- Borrow 1,000 BDT at 10% per year for 3 years.
- Each year you owe 100 BDT interest.
- After 3 years: 1,000 + 100 + 100 + 100 = **1,300 BDT total.**

**Compound interest** is interest calculated on the original amount *plus all previously accumulated interest*. Interest earning interest. The previous year's interest joins the principal next year, and starts earning its own interest.

- Borrow 1,000 BDT at 10% per year compounded annually for 3 years.
- Year 1: 1,000 × 1.10 = 1,100
- Year 2: 1,100 × 1.10 = 1,210
- Year 3: 1,210 × 1.10 = **1,331 BDT**

Difference is small (31 BDT) at 3 years. Now run it for 30 years.

**At 30 years on a 1,000 starting amount, 10% annual:**
- Simple interest: 1,000 + (100 × 30) = 4,000 BDT
- Compound interest: 1,000 × (1.10)³⁰ = **17,449 BDT**

The compound version is **more than 4 times the simple version**. That gap is what we mean by "compounding." It grows exponentially.

### The compound interest formula

> **A = P × (1 + r)ⁿ**

Where:
- **A** = final amount
- **P** = starting principal (what you started with)
- **r** = interest rate per period (as a decimal, e.g., 10% = 0.10)
- **n** = number of periods

You don't need to memorize this. Just understand: every period you multiply by (1 + r). After many periods, that small `(1 + r)` becomes enormous.

For monthly compounding (most realistic for DPS, credit cards, etc.):

> **A = P × (1 + r/12)^(12n)**

For 10% annual rate, monthly compounding, you multiply by (1 + 0.10/12) = 1.00833 every month. Doesn't sound like much. Run it 360 times (30 years × 12 months) and you get... a lot.

### The three variables that matter

Compound interest has three inputs. Each has a different effect:

1. **Principal (P)** — how much you start with. Linear effect. Double the starting amount, double the result.
2. **Rate (r)** — what % per period. Exponential effect on time. 1% extra compounded over decades is enormous.
3. **Time (n)** — how many periods. The most powerful variable. Doubling the time often more than doubles the result.

**The most underappreciated insight in personal finance:**

> *Time matters more than rate. Time matters more than amount.*

Someone who starts with 1,000 BDT/month at age 25 will usually end up wealthier at 65 than someone who starts with 3,000 BDT/month at age 40. The early start beats the larger contribution. Not by a little — by a lot.

## 3. The Rule of 72

There's a beautiful mental shortcut for compound interest. You don't need a calculator. Just memorize this:

> **Rule of 72:** Divide 72 by the annual interest rate (as a whole number) to find how many years it takes for money to double.

Examples:
- At **6%** per year, money doubles in 72 ÷ 6 = **12 years**
- At **8%** per year, money doubles in 72 ÷ 8 = **9 years**
- At **10%** per year, money doubles in 72 ÷ 10 = **7.2 years**
- At **12%** per year, money doubles in 72 ÷ 12 = **6 years**
- At **24%** (credit card) per year, money doubles in 72 ÷ 24 = **3 years**
- At **36%** (high credit card) per year, doubles in 72 ÷ 36 = **2 years**
- At **60%** (chit-fund) per year, doubles in 72 ÷ 60 = **1.2 years**

Use this to sanity-check things in real life:
- If someone offers "your money doubles in 4 years," that's 72/4 = 18% per year. Plausible (high but possible).
- If someone offers "your money doubles in 1 year," that's 72/1 = 72% per year. Almost certainly a scam or a Ponzi.
- If a credit card charges 30%, your unpaid balance roughly doubles every 2.4 years. After 5 years it's 4x; after 10 years it's 16x.

**Try this:** without a calculator, how long does 5,00,000 BDT take to become 10,00,000 at 9% per year?
> 72 ÷ 9 = 8 years. That's it.

## 4. Worked examples

### Example A — The savings story (compounding FOR you)

You decide to put 2,000 BDT/month into a DPS at IBBL earning ~10% per year compounded monthly, for 30 years.

Total you deposit: 2,000 × 12 × 30 = 7,20,000 BDT
Final amount: approximately **45,40,000 BDT**

| Year | Balance (approx) |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 5 | 1,55,000 |
| 10 | 4,12,000 |
| 15 | 8,29,000 |
| 20 | 15,18,000 |
| 25 | 26,46,000 |
| 30 | **45,40,000** |

Look at the curve. In the first 10 years, you accumulate about 4 lakh. In the *last* 10 years (years 20–30), you accumulate about **30 lakh**. The same monthly deposit. The same rate. **Time did the work.**

This is why starting early is everything.

### Example B — The debt story (compounding AGAINST you)

You take 50,000 BDT on a credit card at 30% APR. You make only the minimum payment each month (≈3% of balance). You don't add new debt — just leave that one 50k sitting.

How much you end up paying over 30 years (in just interest, not even principal that hasn't been touched yet):

> Total interest = approximately **15,00,000 BDT**

That's 30x the original 50,000. For a phone or a watch you bought once and forgot about.

This is why high-interest debt is described as "financial cancer." It grows silently, in the background, and the longer it sits, the more it eats.

### Example C — The dangerous one (chit fund)

You owe 1,00,000 BDT to a market lender at 5% per month (60% APR).

If you only pay 5,000 BDT per month — exactly the monthly interest — the principal **never moves**. After 5 years, you've paid 3,00,000 BDT total and you still owe 1,00,000.

If you pay less than 5,000 per month — say 4,000 — the principal grows. After 5 years, it has *increased* to approximately 1,80,000 BDT, and you've already paid 2,40,000 in payments.

**This is why the chit-fund loan is the most dangerous on your debt list, and why even at Stage 0–1 you need to make sure interest is at least covered.**

It's not about "let me ignore it" — it's about "let me at least stop the principal from growing."

### Example D — Time vs amount

Person A: starts saving at age 25. Puts 1,000 BDT/month for 10 years (until 35). Then stops. Never adds another taka. Lets it sit until age 65 at 10% per year.

Total deposited: 1,000 × 12 × 10 = 1,20,000 BDT.
Final value at 65: approximately **22,80,000 BDT**.

Person B: starts saving at age 35. Puts 1,000 BDT/month for 30 years (until 65). Continuously.

Total deposited: 1,000 × 12 × 30 = 3,60,000 BDT.
Final value at 65: approximately **22,60,000 BDT**.

**Person A deposited 3× less and ended up with more.** Because Person A's money compounded for 40 years, Person B's compounded for an average of 15.

> **The lesson: starting early beats saving more. Time is the leverage no amount of cleverness can replicate.**

You're not at age 25 anymore. But you're not at 65 either. The principle still applies — every year you wait makes a real difference. Not "small" difference. **Real** difference.

## 5. Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: Linear thinking about exponential things

Most people's brains naturally think linearly. "Twice the time = twice the result." Compound interest is exponential — twice the time is *much* more than twice the result. This is why people underestimate both the power of saving early and the danger of debt building up.

### Mistake 2: Underestimating small rate differences

"6% vs 8% — small difference, who cares."

Run 30 years on 10,000 BDT:
- At 6%: 10,000 × (1.06)³⁰ = 57,435 BDT
- At 8%: 10,000 × (1.08)³⁰ = **1,00,627 BDT**

A "small" 2% difference becomes nearly 2× over 30 years. Rate matters enormously over time.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring rate when borrowing

People will haggle endlessly over the price of a phone, then sign a loan at any rate. The interest rate on borrowed money is often more important than the price of the thing you bought.

### Mistake 4: Trying to time the market instead of letting time compound

People delay starting their DPS because "the market might dip" or "I'll start when I have more." Every month you delay, you give up some compounding tail. The best time was 10 years ago. The second best time is today.

### Mistake 5: Believing "guaranteed high return" claims

If someone offers 100% return per year, that's saying your money doubles every year. 1 taka becomes 1,024 taka in 10 years. After 30 years, 1 taka becomes 1 *billion* taka. If this were possible, everyone would do it. **It isn't possible.** "Guaranteed high returns" defies the math of compounding — the universe would run out of money. Anyone claiming it is lying.

### Mistake 6: Confusing "what I owe" with "what it will cost me"

If you owe 1,00,000 at 30% interest and you don't pay it down, it doesn't stay 1,00,000. Five years later it's 3,71,000. Ten years later, 13,79,000. Your "1 lakh debt" is actually a "14 lakh debt by year 10 if untouched." Compound interest cuts both ways.

## 6. Try this today (the 30-minute exercise)

### Step A — Compute your DPS scenario (10 min)

You don't have a DPS yet. But play with what would happen if you opened one in Stage 3.

Use the Rule of 72 mentally first:
- At 10% per year, money doubles every ~7.2 years
- So 1,000 BDT becomes 2,000 in 7.2 years, 4,000 in ~14.4 years, 8,000 in ~21.6 years, 16,000 in ~28.8 years

That's per 1,000 BDT, deposited *once* and left alone. Now imagine you deposit 1,000 every month.

Use this rough table for 10% annual return, monthly deposits:

| Monthly deposit | Value at year 5 | Value at year 10 | Value at year 20 | Value at year 30 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 500 BDT | 38,800 | 1,03,000 | 3,80,000 | 11,35,000 |
| 1,000 BDT | 77,500 | 2,06,000 | 7,60,000 | 22,70,000 |
| 2,000 BDT | 1,55,000 | 4,12,000 | 15,20,000 | 45,40,000 |
| 5,000 BDT | 3,87,500 | 10,30,000 | 38,00,000 | 1,13,50,000 |

**Reflect:** even 500 BDT/month — a number you could probably manage even now — becomes over 11 lakh in 30 years. *Without doing anything else.* Just consistency × time.

> Write down: "If I start putting ___ BDT/month into a DPS at Stage 3, in 20 years I'll have approximately ___ BDT."

### Step B — Compute one of your debts compounding (10 min)

Pick your highest-interest debt. Suppose it's the chit fund at 1,90,000 BDT at 5%/month.

The monthly interest alone (if you paid nothing toward principal):
> 1,90,000 × 0.05 = **9,500 BDT/month, just for being indebted**

If you don't pay even that much, the principal grows. Suppose you pay only 4,000/month for a year, and don't add new debt:
- Month 1: 1,90,000 → grows 5% → 1,99,500 → pay 4,000 → 1,95,500
- Month 2: 1,95,500 → grows 5% → 2,05,275 → pay 4,000 → 2,01,275
- ...
- After 12 months: balance ≈ 2,30,000

You paid 48,000 in the year, and the loan grew from 1,90,000 to 2,30,000 — got 40,000 *worse*. That's a 90,000 BDT swing against you in one year.

Now compute the minimum payment to *stop the principal from growing*. (Hint: it's just the monthly interest.)

> Write down: "On my ___ debt, the minimum I must pay each month to stop growth is ___ BDT."

This is the most important monthly number on your debt list.

### Step C — Mental math practice (5 min)

Try these without a calculator. Use Rule of 72.

1. At 9% per year, how long does money double? _______
2. At 12% per year? _______
3. If a friend offers you a "guaranteed 4-year doubling" investment, what rate does that imply? _______ (Is that plausible? _______)
4. Your credit card charges 24% APR. Roughly how often does the balance double if you don't pay? _______
5. A DPS at 10% — if you put in 1 lakh today, what's it worth in ~14 years (two doublings)? _______

Answers: 1) 8 yr, 2) 6 yr, 3) 18% (plausible but high — possible from a good stock portfolio long-term, not "guaranteed"), 4) every 3 yr, 5) ~4 lakh.

## Closing thought

You've now met the most important concept in personal finance. Everything else — inflation, returns, debt, investing — is a *variation* on what you just learned.

Two practical takeaways carrying forward:

1. **Start. Even small.** 500 BDT/month at 10% over 30 years is 11+ lakh. You don't need to optimize. You need to start.
2. **Don't let high-interest debt sit untouched.** Cover the interest at minimum. The math of compounding doesn't sleep just because you're not looking.
