# Day 3 — Inflation + Real Return

Yesterday you learned that money in a DPS can grow to 11 lakh over 30 years. Today you'll learn something uncomfortable: that 11 lakh in 30 years won't be worth what 11 lakh is worth today. By a long shot. Understanding this is the difference between feeling rich and actually being rich.

---

## 1. Why this matters

Imagine your grandfather sometime in 1980, putting 10,000 BDT in a steel box under his bed. He locks it. Forgets about it. You find it 45 years later — in 2025.

The note inside says: "For my grandchildren. 10,000 BDT — a small fortune."

In 1980, 10,000 BDT could buy:
- About 2 grams of gold
- A reasonable motorcycle
- Several months of rent in Dhaka

In 2025, 10,000 BDT can buy:
- About 0.1 gram of gold
- A mid-tier phone (used)
- About a week of rent in Dhaka

The taka note didn't change. The 10,000 number is still 10,000. But the *purchasing power* of that money fell by roughly 90%. Your grandfather thought he was leaving you something. He was, technically. But inflation ate it while he wasn't looking.

**This is inflation.** It's a silent, constant force — like gravity for money. And almost nobody factors it correctly into their financial decisions.

The other concept today — **real return** — is the simple corrective lens that lets you see past the illusion. Once you have this lens on, you'll start noticing that many "good returns" are actually wealth-destroying, and many "safe investments" are quietly losing you money.

---

## 2. The concept

### Inflation (মূল্যস্ফীতি)

**Definition:**
> Inflation is the general rise in prices of goods and services over time. Equivalently: the loss of purchasing power per unit of currency.

If a sack of rice cost 1,000 BDT last year and 1,090 BDT this year, that's roughly 9% food inflation. **Inflation is usually measured as an annual percentage.**

Bangladesh inflation (rough historical average):
- 1990s: ~6%
- 2000s: ~7%
- 2010s: ~6–7%
- 2020s so far: ~7–10% (recent surge driven by global energy + food + currency factors)

For our purposes, **assume Bangladesh inflation is roughly 8–9% per year right now.** Higher than countries like Singapore (1%) or US (3%), lower than Argentina (200%+) or Lebanon.

### Why inflation happens (briefly)

Three main drivers, simplified:
1. **More money chasing same goods** (when the central bank prints / lends too much, or when demand outpaces supply)
2. **Cost-push** (energy / commodities get more expensive, all goods made with them get expensive)
3. **Currency weakening** (Bangladesh's taka has weakened against the US dollar over time — imported goods cost more in taka)

You don't need to master macro-economics. You just need to **assume inflation is always happening** and plan around it.

### Real return (প্রকৃত return)

**Definition:**
> Real return = Nominal return − Inflation
>
> (Or more precisely: Real return ≈ (1 + nominal) / (1 + inflation) − 1, but the subtraction is close enough for most decisions.)

**Nominal return** is what's printed on the label — "this DPS pays 10%."

**Real return** is what you actually gained in purchasing power.

If your DPS earns 10% nominal and inflation is 9%, your real return is:
> 10% − 9% = **1% real**

You feel like you grew 10%. But you only grew 1% in terms of what you can actually buy. The other 9% of your "return" just kept you even with the rising tide of prices.

### The hidden tax of cash

If you keep money in cash (under the mattress) or in a 0%-interest checking account, your nominal return is 0%. So your real return is:
> 0% − 9% inflation = **−9% per year**

You're losing 9% of your real wealth every year you hold cash that isn't growing.

This is the **single most important insight of Day 3:** doing nothing with money is not "safe." It's an active loss of approximately 9% per year. It just feels safe because the *number* doesn't change.

## 3. Worked examples

### Example A — The "savings" that lost money

Imagine your mother put 1,00,000 BDT in a regular savings account in 2010 earning 4%/year nominal. Inflation averaged ~7% over that period.

In 2025 (15 years later) at 4% compounded:
- Nominal balance: 1,00,000 × (1.04)^15 = **1,80,094 BDT**
- Looks like she nearly doubled her money!

But measured in 2010 purchasing power:
- The 1,80,094 in 2025 has the buying power of 1,00,000 × (1.04/1.07)^15 ≈ **65,000 BDT in 2010 terms**

She *lost* 35,000 BDT of real wealth while feeling like she gained 80,000. **This is what happens to people who park money in low-yielding savings accounts during inflation.**

### Example B — The real return of common Bangladeshi instruments

Assume Bangladesh inflation is 9% per year. Let's grade each instrument:

| Instrument | Nominal | Inflation | Real return |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cash (mattress / bKash wallet) | 0% | 9% | **−9%** ⚠️ |
| Standard savings account (Sonali Bank, etc.) | 3–4% | 9% | **−5 to −6%** |
| IBBL Mudaraba Savings | ~6% | 9% | **−3%** |
| IBBL Mudaraba Term Deposit (1-year) | ~7–8% | 9% | **−1 to −2%** |
| DPS / FDR (5–10 yr lock) | ~10–11% | 9% | **+1 to +2%** |
| Sanchaypatra (5-year) | ~11% | 9% | **+2%** |
| DSE blue-chip stocks (long-term avg) | ~12–15% | 9% | **+3 to +6%** |
| International halal ETF (Wahed, ISWD) | ~8–10% USD | 9% BDT* | depends on taka exposure |

*The international comparison gets complex because of currency. For now: most Bangladeshi instruments *just barely* beat inflation. Some lose to it. Cash always loses.

### Example C — The proper way to think about an offer

Suppose a relative says: *"I have a great real estate scheme. You give me 5 lakh today, in 5 years I give you 8 lakh. Guaranteed."*

Sounds great? Let's compute.

Nominal return: (8 / 5)^(1/5) − 1 = 9.86% per year compound

Subtract inflation at 9%: real return ≈ **0.86% per year**

You'd be locking up 5 lakh for 5 years to gain less than 1% in real purchasing power — *if* the deal is real and *if* inflation stays at 9%. If inflation is 10%, you lose. If inflation is 11%, you definitely lose.

**Versus:** a 5-year Sanchaypatra would give you roughly the same real return with zero risk and government backing.

The "great" 8-lakh-in-5-years deal isn't great. It's barely matching inflation. And it carries the risk that the relative defaults. Once you learn to convert nominal to real, all kinds of offers reveal themselves as bad deals.

### Example D — The 30-year purchasing power trap

Yesterday's example said a 2,000 BDT/month DPS becomes 45,40,000 BDT in 30 years at 10% nominal.

Now adjust for 9% inflation:
- Real growth rate ≈ 1% per year
- Real value at year 30 ≈ what 8,80,000 BDT can buy in *today's* purchasing power

So that "45 lakh in 30 years" actually has the purchasing power of about **8.8 lakh today**. Still meaningful — much better than zero — but a far cry from what the nominal number suggests.

**This isn't a reason to not save.** It's a reason to:
1. Save in instruments that beat inflation (DPS > savings account > cash)
2. Eventually move into things with higher real return (stocks, business ownership) once you're past Stage 3
3. Stop being impressed by big nominal numbers without checking the real return

## 4. Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: Thinking 0% is "safe"

Keeping cash in a non-interest account feels safe because the number doesn't go down. But in real terms, it goes down by inflation every year. A "safe" wallet of 1,00,000 today is 92,000 of purchasing power next year — and you didn't notice.

### Mistake 2: Falling for nominal "doubling" promises

"Your money doubles in 5 years!" Sounds amazing.
> Nominal return = 72/5 = 14.4% per year
> Real return at 9% inflation = ~5.4% per year

Better than savings, comparable to stocks, much worse than the hype suggests.

### Mistake 3: Comparing returns from different eras

Your father might remember "I got 13% on my FDR in 1995." But inflation in 1995 was also high. The real return then might have been similar to today's. Don't anchor to nominal numbers from past decades — adjust mentally for that era's inflation.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring inflation in long-term plans

"In 20 years, my kid will need 10 lakh for university." Probably true in *today's* taka. But 20 years from now, university will cost more like 40–60 lakh. If you plan for 10 lakh, you'll be short by 30–50 lakh when the bill arrives.

**The rule:** any future expense estimate should grow at inflation. "I need X today" + 20 years = "I'll need X × (1 + inflation)²⁰ then." At 9% inflation, X roughly **multiplies by 5.6 over 20 years.**

### Mistake 5: Saving in the wrong currency

If you're saving for a future expense priced in USD (like an international trip, foreign tuition, an imported laptop), then BDT savings face additional risk: not just BDT inflation, but BDT-USD weakening. Sometimes saving partly in USD-denominated instruments (or just thinking in USD terms for those goals) makes sense once you're at Stage 4+.

### Mistake 6: Believing "the 9% is just temporary"

Every year, people say "inflation is unusually high right now, it'll go back down." Maybe. But planning your life around "any moment now inflation will return to 3%" is wishful. Plan for what's actually happening, and be pleasantly surprised if it improves.

## 5. Try this today (the 30-minute exercise)

### Step A — Compute the real return of your own money (10 min)

Right now, where is most of your "money sitting around"? Add up everything:
- Cash on hand
- bKash / Nagad wallet
- Bank checking account (probably 0%)
- Bank savings account (probably 2-4%)
- Any DPS / FDR (probably 6-11%)

For each, write the **nominal return** (use a typical rate if you don't know exactly).

Now compute the real return for each: nominal minus 9% (Bangladesh inflation).

Total it up, weighted by how much is in each:

> "My weighted average real return on my current money is approximately ___ % per year."

Most people: this number is **negative**. Don't be surprised. Now you know.

### Step B — The future-cost exercise (10 min)

List 3 expenses you'll definitely have in the future:

| Expense | When | Today's cost | Inflation factor | Real future cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kid's college | 15 years from now | 10,00,000 | (1.09)^15 ≈ 3.64 | **36,40,000** |
| Hajj for you + spouse | 20 years from now | 8,00,000 | (1.09)^20 ≈ 5.60 | **44,80,000** |
| Your retirement income (per year) | 30 years from now | 6,00,000 / yr | (1.09)^30 ≈ 13.27 | **79,60,000 / yr** |

Don't be alarmed by the numbers. They're not predictions of disaster — they're just the math of inflation. The point of computing them is so that when you eventually start planning for these, you plan with the right target.

> Write down 3 of *your* future expenses with adjusted figures.

### Step C — Identify your "real loss" zones (5 min)

Look at your money:
- Anything sitting in cash, bKash, or 0% accounts? → That's losing 9% real per year. Identify how much.
- Anything in a 2-4% savings account? → Losing 5-6% real per year.

> "I am currently losing approximately ___ BDT per year in real terms by holding money in low/no-yield places."

This isn't a guilt trip. It's a measurement. You can't fix what you can't measure.

## 🕌 The Islamic Perspective

Inflation as we know it — a continuous loss of purchasing power in fiat currency — is largely a modern phenomenon, tied to paper money systems. Islam's monetary system was built differently, with several features that addressed inflation centuries before economists named it.

### The gold dinar / silver dirham — built-in inflation resistance

For most of Islamic history, the monetary standard was the **gold dinar** (~4.25g gold) and the **silver dirham** (~2.975g silver). Because the supply of gold and silver couldn't be expanded arbitrarily by a central authority, this system was *naturally* resistant to the kind of currency debasement that drives modern inflation.

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> *"There will come a time when nothing will be of use except the dinar and the dirham."* (Musnad Ahmad, considered authentic by some scholars)

Whatever one thinks of literal interpretations, the underlying principle holds: **commodity money holds value over generations; fiat money usually doesn't**. The grandfather story at the beginning of today's lesson (10,000 BDT in 1980 vs 2025) is impossible under a dinar standard.

### Justice in weights and measures — the Quranic principle

A core economic principle in the Quran is **fairness in measurement and value**:

> *"And give full measure when you measure, and weigh with an even balance. That is the best [way] and best in result."* (Quran 17:35)

> *"Woe to those who give less [than due]. Who, when they take a measure from people, take in full. But if they give by measure or by weight to them, they cause loss. Do they not think that they will be resurrected for a tremendous Day?"* (Quran 83:1–4)

Modern inflation is, in a sense, a *systemic violation* of these principles — currency holders are repaid in units of less real value than what they lent or saved. The Islamic system saw this danger and structurally prevented it through commodity money.

### Ihtikar — the prohibition of hoarding

The Prophet ﷺ explicitly forbade **ihtikar** (hoarding goods to drive up prices, causing artificial inflation):

> *"No one hoards but a wrongdoer."* (Sahih Muslim 1605)

> *"Whoever hoards food for forty days seeking thereby a high price, then he has nothing to do with Allah, and Allah has nothing to do with him."* (Musnad Ahmad)

Modern inflation in essential goods (food, energy, housing) is often driven by exactly this kind of behavior — supply manipulation, speculative buying, monopolistic hoarding. Islam prohibited the supply-side cause 14 centuries before contemporary regulators tried to control it.

### Umar (RA) — the early economic regulator

Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA), as the second Caliph, was famously hands-on with market oversight. He would walk the markets of Madinah at night, inspect weights and measures, set up the **Diwan** (state treasury and accounting institution), and intervene against unjust pricing.

When asked about price-setting, his position aligned with the Prophet's ﷺ — generally markets should set prices freely, but **manipulation, hoarding, and unjust transactions** are to be stopped. This is essentially modern anti-trust + consumer protection law, established 1,400 years ago.

### Zakat — the divine inflation hedge

This is the most beautiful design element of the Islamic economic system regarding inflation:

> *"Take from their wealth a charity (zakat) by which you purify them and cause them to increase."* (Quran 9:103)

Zakat is **2.5% per year on stored wealth above the nisab threshold**. Why annually? Because *wealth that sits is wealth that loses to inflation* — and zakat forces it back into circulation, into the hands of those who will spend it on real goods. The Islamic system thus has a built-in mechanism that:

1. Discourages dead-weight cash hoarding (which loses to inflation anyway)
2. Recycles capital to the productive economy
3. Forces wealthy holders to either invest productively or pay zakat

Zakat is, among many other things, a **counter-inflationary monetary policy** built directly into Islamic law.

### Practical takeaway

You learned today that cash loses ~9% per year to inflation. The Islamic system offers two complementary protections:

1. **Stable money** (historically dinar/dirham; today, allocating to assets that hold real value — productive equity, real estate, or even physical gold/silver as wealth grows)
2. **Zakat-mindset** — don't *let* wealth sit dead. Either it's working (in business, in halal investments, supporting your family) or it's flowing out (zakat, sadaqah, helping others). Money sitting at 0% is doubly painful: it loses to inflation, and it earns no spiritual return either.

The Islamic answer to inflation isn't pessimism — it's **velocity**. Keep wealth moving through productive halal channels.
