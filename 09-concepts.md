# 09 — Finance Concepts Glossary

The 10 foundational concepts to internalize in Stage 0. Plus some that come later. Each is explained in plain English first, then a Bengali analogue + practical example tied to your life.

**How to use this file:** read 1–2 entries per day. Write your own version in the tracker's Notes tab. Tick the concept off in the **Concepts tab** when you can explain it to your spouse in plain Bengali.

---

## 1. Cash flow — নগদ প্রবাহ

**What it means:** How much money comes in and how much goes out, in a given period (usually a month).

**Two simple equations:**
- Cash flow = Income − Expenses
- Positive cash flow = you have surplus at month-end
- Negative cash flow = you're losing ground

**Why it matters:** You can be paid a lot and still be broke if outflow > inflow. Cash flow is the heartbeat. If the heart isn't beating right, nothing else works.

**Your example:** If your freelance income is 70,000/month and household expenses are 65,000/month, your cash flow is +5,000. That 5,000 over 12 months is 60,000 — that's your annual surplus and the source of everything else (savings, investing, debt reduction).

**One-line test:** *Do you know exactly what your cash flow was last month?*

---

## 2. Net worth — মোট সম্পদ

**What it means:** Everything you own (assets) minus everything you owe (debts).
- Net worth = Assets − Liabilities

**Why it matters:** Income is monthly. Wealth is what's left over time. Net worth is the score. It can be negative (yours likely is right now). That's fine — *watching it move toward zero, then positive, is the most motivating financial number that exists.*

**Your example:** If you have 5,000 BDT in cash, 0 in investments, and 7,00,000 in debts → net worth = −6,95,000. Uncomfortable, but it's the starting line. Six months later, with tracking + small income growth, it might be −6,50,000. That's progress that compounds.

**One-line test:** *Can you state your net worth right now in BDT?*

---

## 3. Compound interest — যৌগিক সুদ

**What it means:** Interest earning interest. Or — interest charged on interest. It works in both directions.
- On savings: your money grows on top of money that already grew
- On debt: you pay interest on the interest you didn't pay last month

**The famous quote (attributed to Einstein):** *"Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it earns it. He who doesn't, pays it."*

**Why it matters:** The reason a 60%/year debt destroys lives is compounding. The reason a 10%/year investment over 30 years makes someone wealthy is *also* compounding. Same force, both directions.

**Your example:** 1,00,000 BDT at 10%/year for 30 years = 17,44,940 BDT. Not because someone added money — purely because earlier interest itself earned more interest. The other side: 1,00,000 BDT borrowed at 60%/year for 5 years (unpaid) becomes ~10,48,576 BDT. That's why high-rate debt is so dangerous.

**One-line test:** *Can you explain why 60% APR debt is exponentially worse than 6% APR debt, not just 10x worse?*

---

## 4. Inflation — মূল্যস্ফীতি

**What it means:** Money loses purchasing power over time. Things cost more next year than this year because the value of each taka shrinks.

**Bangladesh today:** Inflation has been roughly 8–10% over recent years. That means 100 taka today buys what about 91 taka of *next year's* taka would buy.

**Why it matters:** If your savings earn 5% but inflation is 9%, you're losing 4% in *real* terms — even though the number on paper went up. Money under the mattress loses to inflation every single year. Money in a 0%-interest checking account also loses.

**Your example:** Save 1,00,000 today. Five years later you still have 1,00,000 nominal — but its purchasing power has fallen to about 65,000 of today's taka. You've lost 35% of value while feeling like nothing happened. *This is why "just save more" without thinking about return is incomplete advice.*

**One-line test:** *Does your savings account beat inflation?*

---

## 5. Real return — প্রকৃত return

**What it means:** Return after subtracting inflation.
- Real return = Nominal return − Inflation
- If a savings account pays 5% and inflation is 9%, real return is **−4%**

**Why it matters:** Headline returns lie by themselves. A "12% return" sounds great until you remember inflation ate 9% of it — leaving only 3% of actual wealth growth. Always think in real terms.

**Your example:** Bangladesh Sanchaypatra pays ~11%. Inflation at 9%. Real return ≈ 2%. That's *good* by Bangladesh standards — but it's only 2% of actual wealth growth, not 11%. Plan accordingly.

**One-line test:** *Can you compute the real return on any investment when told its nominal return?*

---

## 6. Time value of money — সময়ের মূল্য

**What it means:** 1 taka today is worth more than 1 taka next year. Because today's taka can be invested to earn more — and because of inflation eating tomorrow's value.

**Why it matters:** Every financial decision is a comparison: "Should I take 1,000 BDT today, or 1,200 BDT next year?" The honest answer depends on what you can do with the 1,000 between now and then.

**Your example:** Someone offers to "pay you 50,000 in 3 years instead of 30,000 today." If you could invest the 30,000 at 10% over 3 years, it would become 39,930. So the offer of 50,000 in 3 years actually beats taking 30,000 now — by a lot. But change the numbers slightly and the answer flips. *This is how every loan, insurance plan, and investment is properly evaluated.*

**One-line test:** *Can you say, in your own words, why money has time value?*

## 7. Asset vs Liability — সম্পদ vs দায়

**Robert Kiyosaki's simple definition** (from *Rich Dad Poor Dad*):
- An **asset** puts money *in* your pocket
- A **liability** takes money *out* of your pocket

**Why it matters:** Most people get the categories backwards. They call their car, their phone, their house "assets" — but if those things cost them money every month, they're liabilities. Your freelance laptop that earns you 80,000/month is an asset. Your credit card balance is a liability. Your skill is your biggest asset.

**Your example:** Make two lists — what you own that brings money in, what you own that takes money out. Most adults are shocked to discover they own almost no real assets.

**One-line test:** *Name 3 of your actual assets (things that bring money in) and 3 of your actual liabilities (things that take money out).*

## 8. Income vs Wealth — আয় vs সম্পদ

**The difference:**
- **Income** is what arrives monthly (salary, freelance payments)
- **Wealth** is what accumulates over time (savings, investments, assets)

**Why it matters:** High income with zero wealth is a treadmill — you keep running, you never get anywhere. High wealth with modest income is freedom — you can choose how to spend your time. The goal is to convert income into wealth, slowly and consistently.

**Your example:** Doctor in Dhaka earning 5 lakh/month spending 4.8 lakh/month is *less wealthy* than a teacher earning 50k/month spending 40k/month and investing the difference for 20 years. Income flatters today. Wealth defines tomorrow.

**One-line test:** *Are you converting income into wealth at any rate at all?*

## 9. Risk vs Return — ঝুঁকি ও মুনাফা

**The rule:** Higher returns always come with higher risk. There are no exceptions. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling you something or about to take your money.

**The three levels:**
- **Low risk, low return** — savings accounts, Sanchaypatra, government bonds (~5–11% in Bangladesh)
- **Medium risk, medium return** — diversified stock market, real estate (~12–15% long-term)
- **High risk, "high return"** — single stocks, crypto, forex, individual real estate (could be +50% or −90%)

**The lie to recognize:** "Guaranteed 30% returns." Doesn't exist. Either it's risky and lying about the risk, or it's a Ponzi scheme that pays early investors with later investors' money until it collapses.

**Your example:** When you see a Facebook ad promising 30%/month from "forex trading" — you now know two things: (1) forex trading is illegal in Bangladesh, and (2) those numbers are impossible.

**One-line test:** *What's the highest return you should believe is possible from a "guaranteed" investment? (Answer: about the Sanchaypatra rate. Anything higher with the word "guaranteed" is a scam.)*

## 10. Halal vs Haram Finance — হালাল ও হারাম finance

**What it means in practice:**

**Haram (forbidden in Islam):**
- *Riba* (interest) — earning or paying interest on a loan
- *Gharar* (excessive uncertainty) — like gambling, forex speculation
- Income from haram industries (alcohol, conventional banking interest, gambling)

**Halal alternatives:**
- *Mudaraba* — profit-sharing partnership (you provide money, partner provides effort, both share gain/loss)
- *Musharaka* — joint partnership (both provide money + effort, both share)
- *Sukuk* — Islamic bonds (asset-backed, not interest-based)
- *Wadiah / Mudaraba savings* — Shariah-compliant savings (no fixed interest)

**Why it matters to you specifically:** You're working in a Muslim-majority country with a growing Islamic finance industry. Your career path may eventually intersect with this — and personally, knowing the difference protects you from inadvertently participating in haram structures.

**Bangladesh halal banks:** IBBL (Islami Bank Bangladesh), Al-Arafah Islami Bank, EXIM Bank, First Security Islami Bank, Social Islami Bank, City Islami, Standard Bank Islamic.

**One-line test:** *Can you name three halal alternatives to a conventional interest-bearing savings account?*

# More concepts you'll meet later

These don't need to be mastered in Stage 0 — but knowing the *names* helps:

- **Liquidity** — how easily you can turn an asset into cash without losing value
- **Diversification** — not putting all eggs in one basket; spread across assets/sectors
- **Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)** — investing a fixed amount on a regular schedule regardless of price
- **P/E ratio** — Price-to-Earnings; tells you how expensive a stock is relative to its earnings
- **Dividend** — share of a company's profits paid to shareholders
- **Bond / Sukuk** — lending money to a borrower (or its halal version) in exchange for periodic payments
- **ETF** — Exchange-Traded Fund; basket of stocks/bonds you buy as one unit
- **Emergency fund** — 3–6 months of expenses saved for life's surprises
- **Opportunity cost** — what you give up when you choose one option over another

You'll meet each of these in the right stage. Don't rush.

## How to "really" learn a concept (not just read it)

For each concept above:

1. **Read** the entry here
2. **Watch** one 5–10 min video on it (Bengali or English — search the term)
3. **Write** your own one-paragraph version in the Notes tab. If you can't write it, you don't know it.
4. **Teach** it to your spouse over tea. Their questions will reveal what you don't yet understand.
5. **Tick** it off in the tracker's Concepts tab

That cycle — read → watch → write → teach → confirm — is the most effective learning sequence ever devised. Repeat it for each concept.

> **"You don't know a concept until you can teach it to a 12-year-old."**
